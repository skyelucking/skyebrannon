Mon, 02 Apr 2007


january / february / march

January was a lot of negotiating the constructs of a new land locked life that lead to us moving in early February to Irving. All our needs, and more than we could possibly need, are found along a ribbon of street that's lined with the usual neon suspects in a fashion that Charlie and I have agreed is much too much organized. We've vowed to nurture our own little pocket of chaos.
Something else fun was the trip to Body Worlds with Ms. Laura Lopez. It was a very intriguing show, all the insides-out. Sometimes you forgot that these plasticized figures were once bundles of breath and dreams, until you'd see the odd tattoo, or other tell tales of existence. We'd just gotten all kinds of goodies at the art supply store, reaping the benefits of post-holiday sales. I got a man in a box for seventy-five cents, and used my new BH on the sketch to the left.
Charlie and I have been having fantastic times taking advantage of the art and culture Dallas has to offer. We took my mother to the Matisse exhibit at the Dallas Museum of Arts Late nights. We also saw the Kodo Drummers, which rumbled us in our seats and was fantastic.

One of the reasons I came to Dallas was to be close to family. My two young nephews are joys to play with, and their parents (my sister Sam and her husband Ryan) have been wonderful sources of support and suggestion. They are still amazingly productive and social with the two little ones! My brother Zac, a cheerleading coach at SMU, is sending in a second round of try-out videos to Cirque De Soleil. My mother, Mary, is active in her Toastmasters group and has put many a mile on her mini-van coming down to Dallas from Washington, Oklahoma. My father and his wife came into town briefly. He is a Quality Control inspector over the contractors in Iraq who are affixing safety doors to vehicles, to protect soldiers from road-side bombs. We had a very interesting conversation about his ideas of the future of the Middle East region.

I wish I had more time to visit with my family and friends in Oklahoma as well! I'm looking forward to some Easter fun at my Aunt Sole and Uncle Roger's place.

Here are the little men - Brannon Grant on the left and Mr. Joseph Anderson on the right (held by uncle Ty).

For Arizona Statehood Day, I got Charlie admission to a Luthier workshop run by LINT.org - the Luthier Interatives of North Texas. He's been transforming a Christmas tree into a digeridoo of late. That, looking for the right NGO job, cooking fantastic meals, driving me about town while I searched for wheels, and making me smile.

We've been making use of the assorted instruments about the apartment - Indian Tablas, Churango, Ukelele, and acoustic and slide guitars. Charlie also played with the recording equipment a time or two.

The weekends are quick breaths between the weekdays. I’ve dived head first into faintly familiar waters – yet still murky enough to make it a challenging and exhausting swim. A mentor of mine once said of the field I’ve just recently rejoined: “It’s not difficult, it’s just complex.” He’d say it with a wry smile and now I understand the expression so much better. To encapsulate as best I can what my job consists of – I work as a consultant with Cognos products to connect to and build reports off of a company’s data storage places. I enjoy the company I'm with because there is an enthusiasm of somthing small growing larger and room to make an impact. The company trip to Colorado will be nice in June as well!

An upside to this type of work, at least for me, is that the brain actually works better at these tasks if I have something interesting piped into my head. Bless broadband. I have listened to many fascinating lectures from the conference of Technology, Entertainment, and Design (TED), streaming news and podcasts from NPR/BBC, debates, and music shows - Morning Becomes Eclectic in particular.

At the start of March I went to Denver for business and was able to get lots of pleasure in at the same time. I stayed at a fantastic boutique hotel called 'The Curtis' which was all kinds of retro-pop fun. I was also able to visit the Denver Museum of Nature and Science as well as the Denver Art Museum. I did many sketches at the Denver Art Museum, mostly from the African instrument sections. The DAM was so interactive. I started exploring about 7 p.m. and was shuffled out at midnight when they closed.

My favorite sketch of the trip was actually from the DMNS. The Bread and Salt man was carved completely out of colorful combinations of stones by the artist Vasily Konovalenko.

AMY!

Jen!

I was able to meet up for a 'warm the soul' party at my friend Berryman's house. Mulberry wine, chai, and cider were all simmering on the stove, sugar cookies laid in wait for a sprinkle, and an oversize sudoku was there to warm your brain. I hadn't seen Amy in a while, but I knew she'd have a ton of interesting things in the works. She's getting a masters degree in Occupational Therapy (emphasis on brain injury), gave up her car for Lent, and is planning a long journey in 08!

I was also able to meet up with my friend Jen, who I served in Samoa with inthe Peace Corps. Jen's program at Denver U. - the Intercultural Communications and International Relations with an emphasis in Human Rights - was having a potluck and discussion group. It was nice to sense a passion amongst all these world changers and problem solvers! I was happy to meet the Director of the program - an activist who uses her summers to volunteer at a radio station (FIRE) in Costa Rica devoted to offering the viewpoint of women, in regards to war, politics, and other news.

Jen also took me to Boulder for a nice hike through the white snow, under a warm cloudless sky, and a walk about through the center of town.

March has been a great month. I've still been avoiding drink - but I made an exception on St. Patrick's day. We ran the Greenville 5K and spent some QT at Two Rows, a brewery, contemplating what the grego-roman style wrestlers were thinking about mid-position.

Charlie and I have managed a decent attendence to the 24 Hour Fitness .75 miles from our apartment. I'd forgotten how much I enjoy swimming! Charlie got a mountain bike a week or so ago..so I'm all aflutter with excitement to get back on some single track again!

He recently returned from a String Cheese Incident concert with his friend Jeff. Another friend, Dennis, will be getting married in El Salvador. A Cinco DeMayo Wedding in San Salvador, with Charlie as Best man, and me in a festive dress. I can't wait!

I finally got a car. I'd actually done the paperwork to get 3 different cars, but I'll be merciful and spare the details. I've decided that buying a used car ranks in with invasive dental work and black board screeches. After years of not having the freedom of transport, it was nice to move my little two toned 92 Honda Accord through the pulsing Dallas traffice. One morning I'd left behind the baggy that held my breakfast, apples sprinkled with Saigon cinammon, and after work it smelled like someone had baked a pie in my front seat. It was nice.

Charlie and I got to experience a little of the famed SXSW concert series here in Dallas. It was the first time I could maneuver my way to the stage of a concert, and it was fantastic. Andrew Bird plucked his violin, pursed his whistling lips, and did a masterful series of loops to support the belting out of some of my favorite songs. While the band was setting up, I had enough time to sketch this unconcerned bouncer who had half-heartedly told me to stay back.

I’ve actually bound the first draft of the novel in hopes it will allure me to crack it open and begin the messy editing process. At the least, I’d like to send my first three chapters and some query letters before the middle of the year. There are a few other short stories that I’ve started and would like to attend to as well, but time slips through my days so quickly.

So many friends have shared their ideas, thoughts, and writings either through letter, fiction, article, or email. I'm constantly held in the desire to read it all, and respond in kind.

Settling...
I accepted a job in Dallas yesterday and feel as though a small planet (and heavy-orange alien) have been lifted from my to-figure-out list. That list is lengthy and sundry once you've stepped off the plane. And, have you ever tried to figure out small planets and heavy-orange aliens? Not an easy task, believe you me.

So, going forward, I'm going to reconnect with some friends in Oklahoma (I've been so much closer, yet still so far!), meet up with Charlie (and his Bronco full o' instruments) to go to the Feb. 2 Bela Fleck and the Flecktones concert, then we'll head back to Dallas to move into our apartment (still on the to-figure-out list).
When I returned from the Peace Corps, I wanted to 'start being me' right away. However, I couldn't settle right away. I had to be 'me in transition' which is alot more soul wracking and mind boggling than I'd have liked it to be. I moved into my sister's new house (all of us sharing the inaugural night at the cute, cottage style home), into a small room, next to the babies' quarters. I didn't have a car, and my network of friends were all sprinkled across the globe (with not a sprinkle in Dallas - except for Gabriela whom I need to call!)I found myself hoovering over gmail, which made me feel alternately worldly and connected or pathetic and detached.
Going through the motions requisite to settle into the 'real world also triggered my fears of losing that artistic verve and freedom that was afforded me in the lazy Pacific days. So, I'd make it a point to doodle, draw, or write. So, that's still going.
I met up with Charlie, Lola (Laura Lopez), and her boyfriend Sam for New Year's Eve, which was fun. However, it was interesting to see old friends in new context.
I've got a lot more to say..but I need to drive the kid's nanny home..so I'll sign off!

Sun, 01 Oct 2006


*~*~*~*~* S e P t E m B e R *~*~*~*~*
I'll admit it now..August...well, for all my joy for joy and happiness for the day..lots of August was a bit bleh (sans those first few days while Charlie was still here and adventures with friends now and again). Maybe it was those post-vacation-and-visiting-loved-ones-blues. Maybe it was the rubber-club realization that my time is coming so close to an end here, which leaves one altogether relieved and panicky. Maybe it was this, maybe that, or it could very likely be the other. Oh, that other. Or, maybe that month has just paled next to its grand and glimmerous neighbor - September. I've always been partial to September - that ninth month that proves the holidays are indeed happy given how populous a group it is of Virgo babies (of which I'm a member).

Good Eats: It was a month of gastronomic bliss, tempered with a good deal of exercise to allay most of the guilt. Homemade sushi 'n spring rolls at the beginning of the month with a group of Australian friends, Korean food made by Candice, a BBQ at my neighbors, Karen and Peni's , place, some doctored green thai curry I made for the managers of the Bahai temple, the odd visit to Curry House (of course), meals at Visions Restaurant - a mock restaurant open only 3 weeks a year to test the mettle NUS cookery and hospitality students (they passed with flying colors!) - , a funnel cake from the Avanoa Tutusa booth at Teuila Festival, and my birthday meals at Bistro Tatau (the shmanciest place in Apia) and Apa Ula Heights (another ritzy place on a mountain side with gorgeous vistas of mountains and sea).

fru'i'tion - [froo-ish-uhn] –noun 1. attainment of anything desired; realization; accomplishment: After years of hard work she finally brought her idea to full fruition.
2. enjoyment, as of something attained or realized.
3. state of bearing fruit.

I think if, ala Mr. Rogers, I' had a word of the month it be...fruition.

Sept. 5th - My characters made it to the end of their story. I scribbled, in pencil, those two most important words of a novel - The End. It was an odd feeling, not at all what I expected. I liken it to a false peak -an analogy astutely identified by Bryan after I spent time spilling my feelings out about the 'finish'. You think you're about to summit until you realize that end you had your eye on, had worked so hard to get to, although still a great accomplishment with a great view, is not the final one, that there is even more mountain to climb. As time moves on I've gotten more proud about 'finishing' my novel (with those quotes clipped on each side that reveal just how much editing needs to be done) . Mostly it is because of people who are so amazed and supportive by that endeavor of just starting and finishing a story of such length. I'm going to start editing once I return to the states. Yay for 'finishing' draft one of my novel! Woot woot, as my brother says.

Sept. 6th - Teuila Festival. This year's funnel cake booth at the Teuila Fest was a fantastic success, raising upwards of $2500. . I had little to do with the affair, but I did manage to make a funnel cake or two :)
Sept. 8th - Tutti Frutti Pageant. If I only had pictures! The Tutti Fruitti is a fa'afafine (transvestite) pageant which includes categories such as Fruit (you dress like a type of fruit), Formal, Undergarments, and Talent. Roger, a very large fa'af won it. She was Miss Watermelon.

Sept. 17th - I spent a lovely day on Sunday, after the Bahai service, with my artist friend Jill and a few biologists she works with. We went to Aganoa Black Sand beach where Jill amazed me with her brush drawings and I tried my hand at the one above. The scan is a bit darker than the original.
Sept. 18th - I went to a recital held by the June Ryan School of Music, where Selima teaches. It was held in Magik Cinema. I was a bit worried when the MC was introduced (a man of many words) and when he said that there were 64 items on the evenings bill. However, it was a great montage of all ages, talent levels, and pieces (some 15 seconds long, some a minute or two). One piece was called - Frolic in Velocity. Selima looked like she's great with her students and played the guitar in a band piece.

Sept. 20th - 28 begins! I revisit what just-turned-27-Skye had to say:

"I am 27 28. I am in love with life and its sundry opportunities. I recognize the beauty that surrounds, relentlessly tend to the beauty within, and constantly pursue beauty created. I am where I should to be, pursuing the dreams that have both hung 'round my consciousness for a long while and are just now blipping onto my cerebral radar. I am trying to shake away vice and progress towards substance, to recognize the petty from important and trivial from triumphant, to be and be good."

I like the way she thought. I'm keeping it.

One birthday goal I've thus far accomplished: Reclaiming the morning! I've woken up before 6:15 a.m. every weekday. This time has incorporated the activities the I may have made goals of themselves. I read news, write, journal, practice yoga, get to work earlier, and have gone on some runs. I've always been a sunset girl, proudly claiming my night-person-ness. But, this unhurried time in the morning, and those sunrises I've seen on my runs/walks, may just convert me yet.

Sept. 21 - International Day of Peace.
Sept 23rd - Charlie's Birthday!! Charlie's 29th year has been wonderful to read about, to hear about, but most especially, to be apart of. I'm so very intrigued as to what 28 and 30 holds in store for us! I painted the water color above for his birthday. It is based on a photograph he took in Bhutan of some monks at place called 'Tiger's Nest'. Happy Birthday Charlie!!!

Sept. 25th - Phase 1 of the website I've been working on for the Samoa AIDS Foundation went live! For some odd reason a 3 letter domain name is $500USD/yr where a >4 letter name is only $70USD/2 yrs! So, we couldn't get www.saf.ws. but had to use www.safinc.ws (SAF Inc.)Check it out: http://www.safinc.ws/ .

Phase 2 will include a database-driven, push-button update capability so that it can remain sustainable and updated by the SAFstaff. A lot of techy stuff involved in that which I'll be working on in the coming weeks.

Sept. 30th - Almost all of my Saturdays, Sunday nights, and Tuesday evenings in August and September were devoted to working on the first annual 'Battle of the Minds' with the Rotaract Club of Apia. I was the content manager, which meant I compiled the quiz questions given by education professionals in the areas of Arts, Commerce, and Science.

There are so many athletic competitions in Samoa, but very few opportunities for students to show their academic excellence. The show ran for 8 weeks, gaining popularity, and deflecting some criticisms. At its end, though, it proved a very popular and inspiring show! The Prime Minister came to our prize giving!

Counting down the digits: 10 weeks. I've got 10 weeks left on this island, and I'm counting down. I'm so ready to go yet not ready to leave. No, actually, that's not true. I'm ready. I'm really ready to leave. I am enjoying myself, though, as I've been enjoying from the get go.

* ~ J ~ U ~ L ~ Y ~*

July was the coolest month, bringing

Loved ones from their ownlands, making

Memories and stories, stirring

Familiar faces to new smiles....

I've spent much of August wondering how I'd be able to encapsulate such a wonderful month. Well, it will probably happen by skipping over or just skimming the surface of so many happenings. The first half of July was spent with my mother, beginning with an all too brief trip to Sydney followed by quality time in Samoa. The second half was spent with Charlie visiting, from Chile to the place he thought he'd never return, on completely selfish terms because, he reckoned via chat, 'you know... it will be cold here.' Right :) I'm so glad I got to have two of my favorite people so close to me this month of July.

Pre-July -

Me: My mom had a fabulous time in New Zealand. She went to the sky tower and watched jumpers, a salon to get her hair done, walked up and down the plazas, and had some nice food. She got some really fun pictures, too. One of her skydiving, another bungee jumping.

Friend: Oh, wow, how long was she there?

Me: 12 hours.

Hostel dwellers watching the Chris Rock special.

July 2: We arrived out our hostel - The Funk House - nestled in Kings Cross, Sydney's red light district. We'd met a lovely girl, Maria, from Denmark who joined us for my first meal outside of Samoa, fantastic Thai food. The Funk House was full of hippies from all over the world, as hostels tend to be. There was a mural on every wall. If I ever run for office..what a slogan..'A mural on every wall!' Okay, maybe I did spend too much time at the hostel :)

July 3: How could we not? The Sydney Opera House Tour was fantastic.

July 4th - I miss art museums SO much. If you're somewhere where there is an art museum you haven't gone to..go. Stop into the cafe for a cuppa, too. We did. The Museum of Contemporary Art in Sydney had a neat exhibit exploring boundaries and zones. Some were, of course, very abstract. However, many instillation's were videos from people around the world speaking about their own lands being conquered, destroyed, and oppressed through warfare. There's allot I could say about my feelings upon seeing the faces of these people, holding the last remaining slab of rubble not just from their childhood home, but from their childhood neighborhood. But ..encapsulate I will. This isn't that blog entry.

Do you Didgeridoo?

Oh, yeah, we saw Russell Crowe walking down the street on our first day there. I forgot to mention that. Mom wanted to get a picture with him, but he was 'working.' Friends of mine - ask me to tell this story in person.

Here comes the skim, skip, and encapsulate: Mom and I had a fantastic time in Samoa. Highlights were sushi (with Holly 'n Cecilia), tortilla soup (with Iapi, Maka, Bryan, and John), Curry House with the Expats, Siva Afi with Selima, and a Rugby Game.

Sometimes it's not only the someone your with, but the someone you are when your with them. Charlie is one of those people who brings out the best in me. I'm more kind, more curious, and more aware when he's around. I laugh more, think more, exercise more, eat more, and, among others, generally enjoy life more. May seem a bit scary to put it all out there on a website...I'm more brave, too.

We spent this weekend at Lalomanu, a staple Peace Corps retreat spot ran by friendly owners Tai and Sili. Fajitas and sloppy joes, spicy coconut curryless rice, Thrice times to Curry House and and a nice dinner at Apa Ula Heights with Iapi, plus fantastic lunches and breakfast Charlie would make before and whilst I went and taught classes.
Robert Louise Stevenson tomb atop of Mt. Vaea.

Selima let me borrow her guitar,so I got the chance to play some chords to accompany his fantastic solos. 'Your turn!' he'd say and endure my clumsy attempts.
One day we rented a car and cruised the island. Our first stop was Salemumu. Charlie brought 2 bottles of pisco, Chile's favorite liquor, to Samoa. One was for the village father of Preston, another group 70er who is in Chile, who lives in Salemumu. One of my favorite things in the world is to give people good news. Preston's village father was SO touched. It was a highlight! Afterwards Charlie and I went to Paradise beach and relaxed, then had a fantastic meal (it was a theme) at Coconuts resort. A great day all around!
There were a few get togethers during Charlie's visit. There was a very low key BBQ at my house to say goodbye to Mac, Sara, and Ethan. Lots of partying on Saturday, from what we heard! We also visited Charlie's Samoan family. I played with the kiddos, we have a commonality in that we can't resist markers, while Charlie chatted with brothers, aunties, and cousins.

Of course, there is allot more than I've described. Charlie's trip actually extended into August. We got massage appointments before heading to Giadornos. I doodled what's to the left while I waited for his to be done. Just like those short weeks in February, my time with Charlie was a wonderful flash and then done. I'm looking forward to that time and place with Charlie when there isn't the eventual goodbye.

Faafetai tele lava for coming to visit me Charlie!

Alofa tele atu!!

Tue, 11 Jul 2006


*~*~*~J U N E *~*~*~
The sky started a light sprinkle as everyone waited for the last few runners to finish, presenting a brilliant rainbow, and cooling us off.
Sporty Skye - June started with two sporting endeavors. I rode to the wharf and back (46 miles) with a group of friends to kick off the month. It wasn't quite an auspicious beginning, but it ended up a great ride. I also ran a half-marathon (training be damned!) on the 10th. A few other Peace Corps joined me, along with a slew of international runners. I'd trained very hard for the 13.1 mile run last year but fell ill the week before. Nothing was going to stop me this time (even a less than rigorous training regiment)! I'm happy I finished (even if it was slow going).
Hellos - The evening of the half-marathon was also the welcoming party for Group 76. Their fresh faces and enthusiasm made me smile, recalling the way older volunteers had acted toward my own exuberance upon entry into the country. In my post-marathon stupor I managed to make a few batches of fudge: peanut butter, plain, walnut, and hot (cayenne!).
Artsy Skye - At the month's beginning was the Tiapapata Art Center Exhibit, where Helen had a few wonderful pieces in the show. I love going to openings! There was music, great food (Curry House), art, and - best of all - artists, art lovers, and friends. Mid-month I was lucky enough to join Jill in a 'cruise for muse' that lead us to one of the 8 smaller islands of Samoa, Manono. Jill is prolific in her brilliant, quick water color sketches of landscape. I was lucky enough to be encouraged by her as I struggled through my very first.
Goodbyes - I wrote in a letter to Charlie: "It's faamavae season which has left me constantly broke and intermittently drunk." It's true, goodbye season is here. It was a month of lasts for me, mostly celebrated with Nela, but also with others from group 72. We hiked the jungle into the crater Lake Lanatoo. I fed the plump, hiker savvy goldfish that came up in heaving domes of fin over the crusts of bread I threw into the brackish lake water. We also went to a BBQ, danced at Paddles, and had a generally good time being sappy and pontificating about our P cubed - Post-Peace Corps-Plans.
The in-between - the usual reading, guitar practice, and food with friends. I made good on a second attempt at reading Confederacy of Dunces. Two interesting items from his lecture/slideshow:
- He made the point that the best of intentions regarding environmental protection or economic development can be wrong headed. Eucalyptus, a non-indigenous tree, was planted, he thinks, in the hopes to reforest or to get a future crop of logging trees. Samoa Song and Culture

Paul, an older volunteer that has traveled and lived around the world, observed that, unlike anywhere else, Samoans will burst into song. Whether it is the rhythmic beating of a metal drum driving arms to propel a boat through the bay, a graceful Samoan siva, or a sudden chorus coming from my class’ lab computers eleven through fourteen the message and music are loud and clear; Samoans are a people of rhythm, music, and dance.

September 2nd – Island Night - I attended ‘Island Night,’ an evening where students from my school, Samoa Polytechnic, get their boogie on. It is a competition, and with Samoan Idol on the teevee(named in the spirit of that American show, but carried out in more of a Star Search tradition), the students, divided into their ‘majors’, were charged to win. They practiced constantly the weeks before. Take it from a lecturer whose office was next to the ‘Livin Livida Loca’ team. The evening had an International theme. Interesting to me was that yes, the big land masses like India and Mexico were represented, but there was also Tokelau and Niue(two other small island nation). Whichever country, the evening was filled with amazing song, dance, and costumes.
September 5th – TeVaka - The beat of a concert drew me from seclusion, a fact for which I am extremely grateful. TeVaka is a native Samoan who now entertains around the world, colliding Samoan tradition and modern flare.

There was flash from show lights and smoke from the fire dancers. Beautiful Samoan ladies, with healthy bodies and stronger still voices, danced along side a stout TeVaka and his upbeat guitar. The music was fastastic.

September 8th - Samoa 1905 Exhibit - This was an exhibit of photos taken one-hundred years ago by a German meteorologist, Otto Tetens. It was wonderful and interesting to see familiar Samoan spots of land I recognized in such pristine condition, how the missionaries interacted and effected the local population, and the traditional wear that has not changed much in the past century.
September 5th to 9th – Teuila Festival – The concert was a perfect beginning to a week full of Samoan culture displayed at the annual Teuila (Tae – wila) Festival. In all honesty, I mostly busied myself at the Avanoa Tutusa Funnel Cake booth, slinging cakes, touting the promotion of gender and youth equality in Samoa (Avanoa Tutusa’s goals), and raising money to buy creativity supplies for three preschools.

I hope to experience the cultural component more fully next year, as there are dance, chorus, and cricket competitions in the evenings. However, I did look around the booths at the various crafts and plants, food and fabric, and listened to the live music floating through the grounds from a loud PA.

September 10th – Miss Teuila Pageant – I had again planned a quiet evening at home when fortune and intrigue smiled upon me. My friend Cherelle, a journalist with sharp wit and very many opinions, offered another friend Tima and me tickets to the pageant. She had said that they were good seats. Good seats meant in the center two rows behind the judges and one behind the Prime Minister’s vacant chairs!
There were many similarities and just as many differences to American pageants (that I recall anyway). A more modest sarong category was commensurate to the swimsuit competitions, and the Samoan formal wear of puletasi’s which were all beautiful and each wholly different. There was some awkwardness in a few interviews, which have lead to calls for the contestants to be briefed on potential topics.
One of the most interesting portions was the traditional wear and its descriptions. Black represented Samoa in the dark ages before the missionaries and white the light of God. The talent section consisted of traditional Samoan sivas, dramatic dances, keyboard and guitar solos, and a comedy routine and traditional speech. There was a Miss Pineapple, Miss McDonald’s Family Restaurant, and Miss Federal Pacific Insurance. Miss Insurance won.

Fruit salad out front and sea breezes in back…


For the first time in months, my hands felt the cool metal of keys that were my own. One key was for my bedroom – with an ocean facing window and ceiling fan up top. One key was for the gate outside – always locked after the sun’s descent. One key was for the front door – hidden from far away view by flowering tropical plants. One key was for the gate to a private beach – a minute’s walk down a lush grassy pathway.

It’s sea breezes out back and fruit salad in the front yard with banana, coconut, papaya, lime, and mango trees mingling amongst many others. I have a patio already furnished and nestled by my large back yard – with its concrete ring, about 8 feet in diameter, off to the right to hold a garden. Pigs and Chickens and Geckos Oh my!


From the sounds of it, Nu’usuatia is most alive at five in the morning, when the sun pulls above the palms. The horse tied to a tree outside my window exchanges reverberating neighs with another down the road. Individual rooster crows culminate in a roar that sounds from the far distance eerily like the wail of tortured men and from close by gratingly just like a screeching rooster under your window. At half past, the technical tentacles of man slip into Samoa’s Wild Kingdom. The neighs and crows are underscored with the pumping of a radio, booming base of songs I haven’t heard since high school.

Animals are ever-present in Samoa. Pigs are used for food and barter, and penance if the village council, and its ruling matai, has deemed it so. It isn’t uncommon to be fined a sow or two or twenty depending on the infraction, anything from being impolite to eloping. It’s a novelty to us Americans and every day living for a villager. To a Samoan, walking down the village street with out seeing a curious head-cocked pooch, corpulent sow and baby piglets, or line of cheeping chicks would be equivalent to an opening scene of Twilight Zone.

Moving from a first to third world, I’ve realized the extent most Americans are separated from the winged, wild, and wandering four legged. We’ve moved from an agricultural to industrial society, making the separation of man and beast a necessity, such that chickens crossing roads occur only in old jokes or Disney flicks. Aside from farms, quickly being replaced by agribusiness, most people don’t encounter animals that haven’t been given names and cages, or that they aren’t swatting, poisoning, trapping, or hunting. In the U.S. wild animals are the stuff of cable television stations and poorly funded zoos, where the wild has been taken out of the animal shortly after the animal has been taken out of the wild.

A fellow trainee told the story of her farm-family friend whose father bought the remaining chickens from a defunct ‘chicken factory.E Unlike most of the chickens he’d seen, these factory chickens didn’t scatter when he walked toward them. In fact, they sat very still in the same spots they’d been placed after delivery. Confused, they asked the factory foreman about the fowl deal.

It turned out the chickens didn’t know how to walk. From the time they had hatched, they had been in the same small cages sitting along a conveyor belt. The belt system delivered the birdsEfood and water, and carried away their notions to put one talon in front of the other. The farm boy had a new chore of putting bowls of food and water in front of the chickens so they wouldn't starve. Why didn’t the chicken cross the road? It didn’t know how!

No, I’m not sending off a PETA application. No, I’m not abandoning my omnivorous ways, although I do commend those veggies out there. I enjoyed the freshly packed, boneless, skinless chicken breasts, plumped with hormones and sedentary lives Ethat rival some AmericansE- as much as the next first-worlder. I’ve understood for a long time that a simple life is part of an equation, and my positive is someone or something’s negative; that some percentage of every bottom dollar spent oils an unsavory piece of the societal machine. I’m plagued with the ‘Guilt of the Conscientious.EI am trying to even up the sides, though - less guilt and more conscientious- during my Peace Corps service in Samoa and upon my return.

So, when I have the option, I’ll most likely fill my overfull grocery basket with free-range products, free-range turkey for example. I’ll stay optimistic that ‘free-range’ is substantively better than ‘conveyor-belt.’ I’ll do so because I’ve heard stories of chicken factories and seen how man and animal can co-exist, and man still gets fed in the end. It seems to me a wonderful and natural thing, this co-mingling. It has made me think, just maybe, separating the lives of human and beast has made the beast less alive and the human less humane.

Make no mistake; it isn’t all out of Disney’s Cinderella, this coexistence. A gecko frightened me from sleep one afternoon, subletting my ankle with his cold underbelly. As I took my morning shower (under an arced PVC pipe), a worm surfed in with the chilly water, landed, and slithered under my flops along the uneven concrete. There is no blithe co-mingling with the palmetto bugs (a.k.a giant cockroaches), mosquitoes, or ubiquitous ants. But, I must say: It’s nice to see animals running about sans the leashes, cages, sweater sets, or Colonel Sanders - keeping poultry from motion.

Communications in a language you don’t understand can be a bit offsetting, no? O le tali sa’o (correct answer) = ‘Ioe (yes)!!! That’s village life. I was actually fortunate in that four adults and one child are fluent English speakers in my little orange (lanu moli) house (fale). This is not the norm. In fact, some poor souls hear nary an English word once they leave the aoga (school). So when I say I love my Samoan family (Out e alofa lo’u ‘aiga Samoa.) it is as genuine as the Samoan coconuts they serve us for morning tea. They’ve been briefed on immersion learning, so they refrain from speaking English until they can’t stand my dumbfounded expression. Maybe like American matua, they’re afraid it’ll stick. Then they’ll give me a break, translate, and laugh kind-heartedly. I’ve only been in Samoa lua viaso (2 weeks), after all.

The above was my class presentation, giving a speech about my family. Sometimes it is the unfamiliarity with grammer that will put you in embarrassing situations. Sometimes that unfamiliarity can save you. Neither was the case below…

Our third day in the village I was practicing my speech with the family at the dinner table. Only the father and grandmother help Cicilia (another volunteer) and I eat the spread of pulasami (coconut cream in taro leaves), taro, supo (pumpkin, grape tomato, and a varying kinds of meat in a light broth), and i’a (snapper or yellow-tail caught that morning). My tina (mother), aunts, uncles, cousins, and my two brothers sat around the table and on the floor waiting for us to be ma’ona (full).
In the end section they began correcting my grammer, but I was certain I was correct in what I was saying…

Me: E to’alua o’u tuagane. O Berette ma Pandora. (I have two brothers, Berette and Pandora)
Family: Leai. (No)
Me (certain I’ve studied up on this portion of grammer): E to’alua o’u tuagane O Berette ma Pandora.
Family: Leia! Leia!

"Dewey defeats Truman" - an infamous headline that, to this day, is a reminder that Disralei was correct when he, not Mark Twain, said "There are three kinds of lies: lies, damn lies, and statistics." Truman was down in the polls, so a media outlet, the Chicago Daily, erroneously cast Dewey as the victor for their early edition. A media outlet surmising a winner before all the votes were counted?! Glad that doesn't happen these days! Right? Er,yeah.

The Chicago Daily relied on political polls, in lieu of actual votes, to make their ill-fated supposition. I do not have specific details on methodology, but one reason the polls lead the newspaper astray was because their sample was of 'likely voters' taken from vehicle registration records. The problem with this sample was that many poor, rural, and blue collar labor voters were not in this list, and therefore not considered 'likely voters.' Truman, in his famed 'Whistlestop Campaign', reached out to this group, and they responded at the voting booth.



Fast-forward fifty-six years. The 2004 Presidential Election is of no small significance. It's pundits and pollsters to the left and to the right, or maybe from the left and from the right. There is speak of margins of error, soccer moms, Nascar dads, and the 'bounce.' Can we rely on these polls? No Chicago Daily mishaps here, right? Hardly.

It's that tricky 'likely voters' snag. If voter registration numbers are an indication of voter turnout, there may be, be still my heart, an ebb in the apathy. Hip-hoppers have raps that wax political. Poets are prosing to get out the vote. Registering to vote is the new shag cut, the new back tat, the new tribal band. Okay, that may be a bit far. Still, it looks as though sleepy young America has risen from its slumber, at noon after a long night, rubbed the stars from its eyes, and seen a coyote-ugly reality. They want change. And.. They. Are. Not. Likely. Voters.

Just as pollsters of the past were misguided in their sampling from vehicle registration records, today's pollsters are not collecting data from people whose only means of contact are cell phone. Read: young voters.

Don't fret over the polls. Pay attention to the issues, all of them, domestic and foreign. Read your favored candidates plans, Bush or Kerry, and, for kicks, read the other guy's, too. Ignore their rhetoric. The past doesn't matter so much as the future. Don't vote in fear, it clouds better judgment. Vote with your conscious. Vote being future minded. Vote for the sake of posterity. Just vote, and, for Truman's sake, ignore the polls.

Sarbanes/Oxley
The stock market has always been somewhat of a scam. From its very beginning, it was a swindle. Read your history books. Businesses were not required to reveal their business practices or strength. People were expected to put faith in what the CEOs of these companies told them. CEOs lied, often, and took a good many people for their money. The great stock market crash before the Great Depression? A lot of men got very wealthy from that crash, by design.

The crash in 1929 lead to the inception of the SEC (Securities and Exchange Commission). The Commission required the creation of financial statements and internal audits of those statements. However, often the same companies that were preparing financials were also employed to perform audits. There was no separation required.Promises from CEOs were exchanged for promises of consulting companies. A la Arthur Andersen. With this set up, the stock market and integrity of business financials slowly slipped back into the same state of corruption as it had been in the late 1920s. A la Enron.

That is where SOX comes in. Senators Sarbanes and Oxley wrote an act to 'clean up' the practices and image of the stock exchange. It requires internal controls be in place to thwart fraud, with special provisions to ensure there are no conflicts of interest. It doesn't give a plan, mind you, just an expected ends. Many an accountant or compliance officer will tell you that is the hard part, very hard.

Even more harsh are the stakes if a company cannot comply. Accountability is a cornerstone of the SOX Act. We aren't just talking a downgrade of stock class (which is very tough on a company). We are talking prison terms for executive officers, CEOs and COOs etc.. They are not playing around.
