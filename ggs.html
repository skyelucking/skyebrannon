Guns, Germs, and Steel (Jared Diamond) 

      “Opening the paper-back sleeping pill I see.” It was my Mom’s playful comment as I splayed ‘Guns, Germs, and Steel’ (henceforth known as GGS) for my night-time reading ritual. She was visiting Samoa and had witnessed my journeys through the home-stretch of GGS, with their frequent ending dozes.  My page-turned-goal was fifteen, but eventually my cheek rested on the seventh, and my mom would rouse me,”Skye, you’re going to drool on the page, Sweetie. Light up one of those coils and turn out the light.”

      My friend Wikipedia told me that the author’s alternative title would be: "A short history about everyone for the last 13,000 years". The playful (albeit trite, ask Hawkings and Bryson) title for an  expansive subject in a slimlined text was a good and truthful one. Diamond reviews what factors led some civilizations to thrive while others fell subject to domination. He argues that these fates of societies were largely due, not to specific congnitive or racial factors but, opportunities the local lands and beasts provided. There are lots and lots of examples of how agriculture and the gregariousness of large animals lead societies toward their separate fates.

      Although, as I state above, the last few pages were a yawn-coaxing rehash, what they were rehashing, when served piping fresh, was extremely interesting. The 1998 Pulitzer Prize committee did well when they awarded Diamond their honor. The book is filled with examples that ellicit: “Makes sense,” and  “Huh, never thought that would make such a difference, but sure enough…,” and “Interesting.” For example: wild stalks of wheat, back in the day, were programmed to shatter and spread their grain-seeds at the first opportinity. That’s the evolutionary agenda – to keep on keepin’ on. It was only the mutants that held onto their grains long enough for a hungry, bagel-crazed human to harvest and cultivate them for the future. Ironically it is the ‘evolutionary-smart’ wheat that is now sparse and the product of ‘evolutionary-dummies’ we’re spreading lox on today. Makes sense. We eat the mutants (and are still mutating away at them). Interesting.

       There are more volunteers than one could count on a single hand that have read GGS. The book strings together history, biology, agriculture, anthropology, genetics, psychology, geography and a few other sciences I’m cetain I’m slighting. With this grab-bag of sciences and the numerous examples in Diamond’s book, it is so very easy to relate or reference it in a many and wonderous conversation.  Yes, as I mentioned, the abundant examples do wear on you a bit at the end, but the book on the whole is worth it. My advice is to definitly read “Guns, Germs, and Steel” but, for the sake of dry pages, save its last chapter for the daytime. 

      

Skye Brannon
